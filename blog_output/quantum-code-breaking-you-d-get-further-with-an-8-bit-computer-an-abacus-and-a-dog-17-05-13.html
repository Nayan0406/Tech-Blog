**Quantum Code Breaking? You'd Get Further with an 8-bit Computer, an Abacus, and a Dog**

The world of cryptography is abuzz with the looming threat of quantum computers, touted to break even the most secure encryption algorithms. In response, the US National Institute for Standards and Technology (NIST) has been spearheading an effort to develop post-quantum cryptographic algorithms since 2016. But not everyone is convinced of the urgency or feasibility of this endeavor. In fact, computer scientist Peter Gutmann recently told The Register that the whole notion of quantum code breaking is, well, "bollocks". So, what's behind this skepticism, and is Gutmann's assessment a breath of fresh air or a dose of cynicism?

For context, NIST's push for post-quantum cryptography is rooted in the fear that quantum computers, with their exponentially greater processing power, could potentially crack even the most secure encryption algorithms currently in use. This has led to a flurry of research and development in the field, with various approaches being explored, from lattice-based cryptography to code-based cryptography. However, Gutmann argues that this effort is largely misplaced, citing the significant technical hurdles that still need to be overcome before quantum computers can even begin to pose a threat to encryption.

Gutmann's skepticism is rooted in the harsh realities of quantum computing. As he points out, even the most advanced quantum computers today are still plagued by error rates that make them unusable for practical applications. Moreover, the sheer complexity of scaling up these systems to tackle complex encryption algorithms is daunting, to say the least. "You'd get further with an 8-bit computer, an abacus, and a dog," Gutmann quipped, highlighting the absurdity of the notion that quantum computers are just around the corner, ready to break our encryption. His assessment is a sobering reminder that, despite the hype, we're still far from a quantum computing revolution.

So, what does this mean for the future of cryptography? While Gutmann's comments may seem dismissive, they also serve as a much-needed reality check. Rather than getting caught up in the frenzy of post-quantum cryptography, perhaps we should be focusing on more pressing security concerns, like the woeful state of encryption implementation in many organizations. By taking a step back and assessing the actual risks and threats, we may just find that our energy is better spent on more tangible, immediate security challenges. As Gutmann's comments so eloquently put it, sometimes, a dose of skepticism can be a powerful catalyst for progress.
